## 1. What is Project Budgeting (in plain English)

Imagine you're planning a wedding. You don't just say "let's do a wedding" — you ask:

- How much money do I have? (Total budget)
- What does each piece cost? (Venue, catering, photography, attire — line items)
- What if something goes wrong? (Buffer for surprises — contingency)
- How do I track that I'm not overspending? (Tracking actuals vs plan)
- What do I do when the caterer's price goes up? (Variance management & re-baselining)

**Project budgeting is exactly this — but for a business deliverable.** It's the discipline of:

> **Estimating, allocating, monitoring, and controlling all the money required to deliver a project on time, on scope, and on quality.**

A project manager who can't manage a budget is like a pilot who can't read fuel gauges. You may take off, but you won't land where you planned.

### The 3 fundamental questions a PM must answer at any moment:

1. **How much was this project supposed to cost?** (Baseline budget)
2. **How much have we actually spent so far?** (Actuals)
3. **How much will it cost to finish it?** (Estimate at Completion — EAC)

Master these three and you've already crossed 70% of what hiring managers care about.

## 2. Why it matters

### From a business perspective:
- **Cash flow planning** — companies need to know when money will leave their accounts.
- **ROI justification** — every project must prove its return is worth its cost.
- **Resource allocation** — money tied up in one project can't fund another.
- **Stakeholder trust** — board members and clients trust PMs who control numbers.

### From a career perspective:
- It's the **#1 differentiator** between a Junior PM and a Senior PM.
- Most PM interviews include at least one budget-related scenario.
- Quantified budget achievements ("Delivered $2.3M project 8% under budget") are the resume bullets recruiters circle.
- It's a **prerequisite for PMP, PRINCE2, and PgMP** certifications.

## 3. The Glossary

Memorize these. They will appear in interviews verbatim.

| Term | Plain-English meaning |
|---|---|
| **Cost Estimate** | An educated guess of what something will cost before work begins. |
| **Budget** | The approved version of cost estimates, allocated across time. |
| **Baseline (Cost Baseline)** | The locked-in original budget — your reference point. Cannot be changed without formal approval. |
| **Actuals** | What you've actually spent so far. |
| **Forecast / EAC (Estimate at Completion)** | What you now expect the total project to cost, given what you know today. |
| **Variance** | The difference between plan and actuals. Negative = over budget. |
| **Contingency Reserve** | Money set aside for *known* risks (~5–15% of budget). PM controls this. |
| **Management Reserve** | Money set aside for *unknown* risks. Senior management controls this. |
| **Direct Costs** | Costs that exist *because* of the project (developer salaries on the project, software licenses bought for it). |
| **Indirect Costs** | Shared overheads (office rent, electricity, IT support) allocated to the project. |
| **Capex (Capital Expenditure)** | Money spent on long-term assets (servers, buildings, software platforms). |
| **Opex (Operating Expenditure)** | Day-to-day running costs (subscriptions, salaries, utilities). |
| **Burn Rate** | How fast a project is spending money per week/month. |
| **CAPEX vs OPEX classification** | A finance team will always ask which bucket your costs fall into — it affects taxes and accounting. |
| **WBS (Work Breakdown Structure)** | A tree that breaks the project into deliverables; you cost-estimate at the leaf level. |
| **Bottom-Up Estimating** | Estimate each small task, then add up. Most accurate, slowest. |
| **Top-Down Estimating** | Senior leadership decides total budget; you fit work into it. Fastest, least accurate. |
| **Analogous Estimating** | "Last year's similar project cost $X — this one will be similar." |
| **Parametric Estimating** | Use a formula: e.g., $50/hour × 800 hours = $40,000. |
| **Three-Point Estimating** | (Optimistic + 4×Most Likely + Pessimistic) / 6 — known as PERT formula. |
| **Earned Value (EV)** | The dollar value of work *actually completed* so far. |
| **Planned Value (PV)** | The dollar value of work *that should have been completed* by now. |
| **Actual Cost (AC)** | What you've actually spent so far. |
| **CPI (Cost Performance Index)** | EV / AC. Above 1.0 = under budget. Below 1.0 = over budget. |
| **SPI (Schedule Performance Index)** | EV / PV. Above 1.0 = ahead of schedule. Below 1.0 = behind. |
| **Cost Variance (CV)** | EV − AC. Negative = over budget. |
| **Schedule Variance (SV)** | EV − PV. Negative = behind schedule. |
| **Sunk Cost** | Money already spent that can't be recovered — should NOT influence future decisions. |
| **Re-baselining** | Formally resetting the original budget after a major scope or risk change. Requires sign-off. |

## 4. BEGINNER LEVEL

### How to build a project budget — the 7-step method

#### **Step 1: Define the scope clearly**
You cannot budget what you cannot describe. Start with:
- A one-page project charter
- A Work Breakdown Structure (WBS) — break the project into deliverables, then sub-deliverables, then tasks.

> **Rule:** If a task is too big to estimate in hours, break it down further.

#### **Step 2: Identify all cost categories**
Typical buckets for a software/IT project:

| Category | Examples |
|---|---|
| **Labor / People** | Internal salaries, contractor day-rates |
| **Software & Tools** | Licenses, SaaS subscriptions, cloud (AWS, Azure) |
| **Hardware** | Laptops, servers, network gear |
| **Travel** | Flights, hotels, per diem |
| **Training** | Courses, certifications for the team |
| **Vendors / Third-party** | Outsourced design, consulting, audits |
| **Facilities** | Office space, meeting rooms |
| **Contingency** | Buffer for risks |

#### **Step 3: Estimate each line item**
Choose a method per item:
- Use **analogous** (compare to past) for high-level stuff
- Use **parametric** (rate × quantity) for predictable items like labor
- Use **bottom-up** for the critical path / largest cost lines
- Use **three-point (PERT)** for uncertain items

**Worked example — three-point estimate:**
> A developer thinks the API integration will take:
> Optimistic = 80 hrs · Most Likely = 120 hrs · Pessimistic = 200 hrs
>
> PERT = (80 + 4×120 + 200) / 6 = (80 + 480 + 200) / 6 = **126.7 hours**
>
> At $75/hour blended rate = **$9,500**

#### **Step 4: Add Contingency Reserve**
Typical industry ranges:
- Low-uncertainty projects: 5–10%
- Medium-uncertainty: 10–15%
- High-uncertainty (R&D, new tech): 15–25%

> **Don't bury contingency inside line items** — keep it visible as its own line. That's what professional PMs do.

#### **Step 5: Add Management Reserve**
Usually 5–10% on top, controlled by the sponsor/PMO, not the PM.

#### **Step 6: Time-phase the budget**
Spread costs across months — this is the **cost baseline curve**, often called the **S-curve** because cumulative spending tends to look like a stretched "S".

| Month | Labor | Software | Travel | Total | Cumulative |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Jan | $40,000 | $5,000 | $2,000 | $47,000 | $47,000 |
| Feb | $60,000 | $5,000 | $3,000 | $68,000 | $115,000 |
| Mar | $80,000 | $5,000 | $4,000 | $89,000 | $204,000 |

#### **Step 7: Get formal approval — and lock it**
Once stakeholders sign off, this becomes the **Cost Baseline**. From this moment forward, every change is tracked as a variance.

## 5. INTERMEDIATE LEVEL

This is where most candidates separate themselves in interviews.

### A. The Earned Value Management (EVM) Triangle

EVM answers: **"Are we on budget AND on schedule, right now?"**

Three numbers, used together:

| Metric | Formula | What it tells you |
|---|---|---|
| **PV (Planned Value)** | % of work planned × Total Budget | "By today, we should have completed $X worth of work" |
| **EV (Earned Value)** | % of work actually done × Total Budget | "We've actually completed $Y worth of work" |
| **AC (Actual Cost)** | Real spending so far | "We've actually spent $Z" |

#### Worked Example
You have a $100,000 project planned over 10 weeks.
- After Week 5, you should be 50% done → **PV = $50,000**
- You've actually completed only 40% → **EV = $40,000**
- You've spent so far → **AC = $55,000**

Now compute:

| Index | Formula | Result | Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Cost Variance** | EV − AC | 40k − 55k = **−$15,000** | Over budget by $15K |
| **Schedule Variance** | EV − PV | 40k − 50k = **−$10,000** | Behind schedule by $10K worth of work |
| **CPI** | EV / AC | 40k / 55k = **0.73** | Getting only 73 cents of value per dollar spent |
| **SPI** | EV / PV | 40k / 50k = **0.80** | Working at 80% of planned pace |
| **EAC (Estimate at Completion)** | BAC / CPI | 100k / 0.73 = **$137,000** | Project will likely cost $137K total at this rate |

> **If you can walk an interviewer through this calculation cold, you instantly look senior.**

### B. Variance Analysis & Corrective Action

When you see negative variance, the senior PM move is:
1. **Identify the root cause** (not the symptom). Is it scope creep? Estimation error? Resource churn?
2. **Quantify the impact** on EAC.
3. **Present 2–3 options** to the sponsor with trade-offs:
   - Option A: Reduce scope → save $X, deliver on time
   - Option B: Add resources → keep scope, cost +$Y
   - Option C: Extend timeline → keep scope and budget, ship 3 weeks late

> Never present a problem without options. That's the difference between a PM and a senior PM.

### C. Contingency Drawdown Tracking
Track contingency separately. A common dashboard view:

| Week | Contingency Used | Contingency Remaining | % Project Complete |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1–4 | $0 | $50,000 (100%) | 25% |
| 5–8 | $12,000 | $38,000 (76%) | 50% |
| 9–12 | $20,000 | $18,000 (36%) | 65% ⚠ |

If your contingency burn outpaces your % completion — escalate early.

### D. Cost Forecasting Methods

| Method | When to use |
|---|---|
| **EAC = BAC / CPI** | When current performance is expected to continue |
| **EAC = AC + (BAC − EV)** | When the rest of the project will run as originally planned (one-off issue) |
| **EAC = AC + Bottom-up ETC** | When you can re-estimate remaining work in detail |

## 6. ADVANCED LEVEL

### A. Portfolio & Program Budgeting
- A **Program Manager** allocates budgets *across* multiple projects.
- A **PMO** balances which projects get funded based on strategic alignment, ROI, and risk.
- Tools: capital allocation matrices, NPV (Net Present Value), IRR (Internal Rate of Return), payback period.

> Know how to compute **NPV** at a basic level. NPV = sum of (Cash Flow / (1+r)^t) − initial investment. If NPV > 0, the project creates value.

### B. Rolling-Wave Budgeting
Used in long programs. You budget the next 6–12 months in detail, and later months in broad strokes. As work progresses, you "roll the wave" forward — adding detail to what's now coming up.

### C. Zero-Based Budgeting
Every cost must be re-justified from zero each cycle, instead of "last year +5%". Common in cost-cutting environments.

### D. Activity-Based Costing (ABC)
Allocates indirect costs (shared services, overhead) to projects based on usage. Critical when you're reporting profitability per project.

### E. Re-baselining
When scope/schedule changes are so large that the original baseline is no longer meaningful. Requires:
1. Formal Change Request
2. Sponsor / Steering Committee approval
3. Updated cost baseline, schedule, and risk register
4. Communication to all stakeholders

> **Rule:** Don't re-baseline because you missed a number. Only re-baseline because the project itself has fundamentally changed.

### F. Financial Governance & Stage Gates
Senior PMs run budgets through gates:
- **Gate 0:** Concept approved → small discovery budget released
- **Gate 1:** Business case approved → Phase 1 budget released
- **Gate 2:** Design complete → build budget released
- **Gate 3:** Build complete → deployment budget released

This protects the company from runaway spending on bad ideas.

### G. Vendor & Contract Cost Management
Senior PMs handle:
- **Fixed Price** contracts — vendor takes the risk
- **Time & Material (T&M)** — you take the risk, but flexible
- **Cost-Plus** — vendor charges costs + fee
- **Not-to-Exceed (NTE)** caps — protect against runaway T&M

## 7. Waterfall vs Agile Budgeting

| Aspect | Waterfall | Agile |
|---|---|---|
| **Budget set** | Up front, full project | Per release / quarter / sprint |
| **Scope** | Fixed | Variable |
| **Best metric** | EVM (CPI/SPI) | Cost per Story Point, Velocity-based forecast |
| **Re-baselining** | Rare, formal | Continuous (each PI / quarter) |
| **Reporting** | S-curve, milestone reports | Burn-up / burn-down, release burn |

### Agile-specific budgeting concepts
- **Beyond Budgeting** — fund teams continuously, not projects.
- **Lean Portfolio Management (LPM)** — SAFe's approach: fund value streams instead of projects.
- **Capex/Opex split in Agile** — capitalize feature work that creates new functionality; expense maintenance.

## 10. Interview Prep

### A. The 12 most-asked budget questions, with model answers

**Q1. How do you build a project budget from scratch?**
> Walk through the 7-step method: charter → WBS → cost categories → estimate each item (mention bottom-up, parametric, three-point) → contingency → time-phase into S-curve → baseline approval. Add: "I always make contingency a visible line, never hidden in estimates."

**Q2. What's the difference between contingency reserve and management reserve?**
> Contingency = for KNOWN risks I've identified, owned by me as PM. Management reserve = for UNKNOWN risks, owned by the sponsor. Both protect the project, but only contingency is part of my baseline.

**Q3. Your project is 50% through and 25% over budget. What do you do?**
> Use STAR. *Situation:* spotted CPI of 0.80 at the midpoint. *Task:* recover or transparently re-plan. *Action:* (1) root-cause variance — was it estimation, scope creep, vendor cost, or productivity; (2) compute EAC under multiple scenarios; (3) prepare 3 options for sponsor — reduce scope, add resources, extend timeline; (4) document a Change Request. *Result:* sponsor selected option B; project closed at 6% over budget instead of projected 22%.

**Q4. What is Earned Value Management and when do you use it?**
> EVM is a method to measure project performance using three numbers — Planned Value, Earned Value, and Actual Cost — that tell me cost AND schedule status simultaneously. I'd use it on any project over [budget threshold] or where the sponsor needs predictable forecasting. Walk through CPI/SPI computation if asked.

**Q5. Walk me through a CPI calculation.**
> CPI = EV / AC. If I've completed $40K worth of planned work but spent $50K, my CPI is 0.80 — meaning I'm getting 80 cents of value per dollar spent. Below 1.0 = over budget. I'd use this number to project EAC = BAC / CPI.

**Q6. How do you estimate when there's high uncertainty?**
> Three-point (PERT) estimating combined with bottom-up where possible. I document my assumptions clearly so they can be revisited. I bias toward larger contingency (15–25%) and frequent re-forecasting (every 2 weeks instead of monthly).

**Q7. How do you handle scope creep?**
> Tight change-control process. Every change request must include cost, schedule, and risk impact. Sponsor approves before work begins. I never absorb scope without approval — that's the #1 cause of silent budget overruns.

**Q8. Tell me about a project where you saved money.**
> Use STAR with specific numbers: "Renegotiated a vendor contract from T&M to fixed-price after Phase 1, saving $X..." Show that cost optimization is a discipline, not a one-time event.

**Q9. How do you report budget status to executives?**
> One slide. Three numbers: BAC, AC-to-date, EAC. CPI/SPI as health indicators (RAG). Then risks to the forecast. Executives have no time — they need answers in 30 seconds, not raw spreadsheets.

**Q10. What's a sunk cost and why does it matter?**
> Money already spent that I can't recover. It must not influence go/no-go decisions. The right question is "Given today's information, is the remaining investment worth the remaining benefit?" — never "we've already spent $X, we have to keep going."

**Q11. How do Agile projects budget differently?**
> Agile typically funds teams or value streams, not full projects up front. Budget is set per release/quarter; scope flexes. Tracking shifts from CPI to velocity-based forecasting and burn-up charts. Capex/Opex classification still matters and is a real conversation with Finance.

**Q12. What would you do if your project ran out of contingency at 60% complete?**
> Three things in order: (1) Stop and root-cause why contingency burned faster than completion. (2) Quantify the gap honestly — what's the new EAC? (3) Escalate to sponsor with options before continuing — request management reserve, descope, or formally re-baseline. Hiding this is career suicide.

### B. Behavioral Q&A frame (STAR)

For any "tell me about a time" question, use:
- **S**ituation — context, scale, stakes
- **T**ask — what you owned
- **A**ction — *specific* steps you took (use "I", not "we")
- **R**esult — quantified outcome ("delivered 8% under budget")

### C. Questions to ASK the interviewer (signal seniority)
- "What's the typical project size and budget range I'd be managing?"
- "How are budgets governed here — project-based or value-stream / LPM?"
- "What tooling does the PMO use for cost tracking and EVM?"
- "How does Finance partner with PMs on Capex/Opex decisions?"

## 11. Common Mistakes

Avoid these — they're the silent career-killers.

1. **Hiding contingency inside line items** — when you need it, no one knows it's there.
2. **Reporting "actuals = budget"** without analyzing variance — that's bookkeeping, not project management.
3. **Confusing "cost" with "value"** — cheap doesn't mean good.
4. **Not tracking commitments** — money owed but not yet paid is still spent.
5. **Letting sunk costs drive decisions** — see Q10.
6. **One-time estimates, never re-forecast** — your budget is alive, not a museum piece.
7. **Saying "we'll figure it out"** — interviewers and executives both hear "I don't have a plan."
8. **Quantifying with vague words** ("a lot", "significantly") — always use real numbers.
9. **Owning the budget alone** — PMs partner with Finance, PMO, and the sponsor.
10. **Re-baselining to hide poor performance** — re-baseline only for genuine scope changes.

## 12. 30-Day Plan

A focused, layman-to-conversational ramp.

### Week 1 — Concepts (≈ 5–6 hrs)
- [ ] Watch: PMI's "What is Project Cost Management" overview videos

- [ ] Memorize: the glossary in Section 3

### Week 2 — Estimating & Building Budgets (≈ 6–8 hrs)
- [ ] Practice: bottom-up estimate for a hypothetical "build a website" project
- [ ] Practice: three-point (PERT) on at least 5 line items
- [ ] Build: a $250K project budget template in Excel (download templates from PMI/Smartsheet)

### Week 3 — EVM & Variance (≈ 6–8 hrs)
- [ ] Walk through 5 worked EVM examples (compute PV, EV, AC, CPI, SPI, EAC)
- [ ] Watch: Mike Clayton or Ricardo Vargas videos on EVM (links below)
- [ ] Build: an EVM dashboard in Excel — input AC and % complete, output CPI/SPI/EAC
- [ ] Take: a free PMP-style quiz on cost management (PM Exam Simulator, Edusys, Master of Project Academy)

### Week 4 — Real-world & Interview Prep (≈ 6–8 hrs)
- [ ] Practice answering all 12 interview questions out loud, ideally recorded
- [ ] Customize 4–6 resume bullets with your real numbers
- [ ] Read: 2 case studies on portfolio budgeting / re-baselining (Harvard Business Review free articles, PMI case study library)
- [ ] Optional: register for PMI free webinars or a Coursera "Budgeting and Scheduling Projects" module

After 30 days you will be able to confidently discuss budgeting at a senior PM level in interviews.

## Final Note

Budgeting is the single highest-leverage skill in project management because it is the language **finance, executives, and clients all share**. Once you can talk about a project in terms of CPI, EAC, contingency burn, and stage-gate funding — you stop sounding like a coordinator and start sounding like a leader.

Start with Week 1 of the plan. Build a tiny budget in Excel for something real (even a personal goal). Compute one CPI value. The vocabulary becomes yours faster than you expect.

> *The best PMs aren't the ones who never go over budget. They're the ones who see it coming three weeks earlier than anyone else, and walk into the sponsor's office with three options — not one problem.*
